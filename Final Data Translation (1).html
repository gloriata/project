The COVID-19 pandemic has had widespread and far-reaching effects on the global economy, with the retail sector experiencing particularly significant disruptions. While our company has been able to monitor its own performance during this period, we wish to understand the broader economic impacts, both within retail and across other industries.

To support our investigation into the economic effects of the COVID-19 pandemic, our team analyzed data from the Current Population Survey (CPS) provided by IPUMS, with a focus on employment trends from the pre-pandemic period through April 2022. By examining shifts in employment at both the individual and industry levels, our goal is to generate meaningful insights into the evolving labor market landscape and assess the performance of the retail sector in comparison to other segments of the economy.

Our analysis is structured around three central research questions:

  1. How has COVID-19 affected the health of the retail industry, as measured by employment?
  2. How has retail fared relative to other industries?
  3. What has changed about who is working and earning income during the pandemic?

Our primary goal is to measure how employment and unemployment in the retail sector changed due to COVID-19. To do this, we need to:

To assess the impact of COVID-19 on retail employment, we estimated linear regression models with employment and unemployment levels (in millions) as dependent variables. The key explanatory variable, post_covid, is a binary indicator equal to 1 for periods from April 2020 onward, capturing the pandemic’s onset. We also included a linear time trend (month_index) to control for underlying trends over the study period.

Results from the employment model show a statistically significant negative coefficient on post_covid (-1.027, p < 0.01), indicating that retail employment decreased by approximately 1.03 million jobs immediately after the pandemic began, holding the time trend constant. The positive but marginally significant coefficient on month_index (0.0214, p ≈ 0.07) suggests a slight upward trend in employment over time, consistent with a gradual recovery.

In contrast, the unemployment model reveals a significant positive coefficient on post_covid (1.270, p < 0.001), indicating an increase of roughly 1.27 million unemployed retail workers immediately following the pandemic’s onset. The negative and highly significant time trend coefficient (-0.0296, p < 0.001) reflects a steady decrease in unemployment over time after the initial spike.

The models explain a moderate portion of the variation in employment (Adjusted R² ≈ 0.14) and a more substantial proportion in unemployment (Adjusted R² ≈ 0.51).

These findings confirm that COVID-19 caused a sharp and immediate drop in retail employment alongside a sharp increase in unemployment, followed by signs of gradual recovery over the subsequent months.

After controlling for month, state, age, race, and citizenship status, the probability that a retail worker is employed decreased by about 2.9 percentage points following the onset of COVID-19 (April 2020 and onward). Assumptions and Their Plausibility:

  1. Parallel trends: We assume that absent COVID-19, retail employment would have continued its prior trend. The month index in our regressions helps control for time trends, but structural breaks unrelated to COVID-19 could bias estimates. The smooth pre-pandemic trends in the plots support this assumption.
  2. Exogeneity of post-COVID indicator: While government stimulus or policy changes may also impact employment, we assume no other major confounding event starting precisely April 2020 influences retail employment.
  3. Accurate classification: The use of industry codes and employment status from CPS is assumed accurate and consistent over time. Some misclassification or reporting errors could exist but are unlikely to systematically bias the results.

Summary: Our analyses show that the COVID-19 pandemic caused a sharp and statistically significant decline in retail employment and a simultaneous spike in unemployment starting April 2020. While retail employment shows signs of recovery, it remains below pre-pandemic levels as of April 2022. Controlling for demographics and seasonality confirms the robustness of these findings.

In this difference-in-differences (DiD) mode, the dependent variable is total employment in millions (total_employed_mil), modeled as a function of:

  • post_covid: an indicator for the period after April 2020 (pandemic onset),

  • month_index: a linear time trend to control for underlying temporal trends in employment.

Results:

  • Non-retail employment dropped significantly by about 11.07 million (post_covid coefficient), holding other factors constant.

  • The positive and statistically significant interaction term (5.446 million) indicates that retail employment experienced a smaller employment decline relative to non-retail industries during the pandemic period.

Assumptions:

  1. Parallel Trends Assumption (for DiD): Our DiD model compares changes in employment between retail and non-retail sectors before and after April 2020. A critical identifying assumption is that, in the absence of COVID-19, employment trends in both groups would have followed parallel paths over time. The employment trend plot (see Employment Trends: Retail vs. Non-Retail) shows that both groups had relatively stable and similar trajectories before April 2020. While not a formal test, the visual evidence supports the plausibility of the parallel trends assumption.
  2. Given the time-series structure of the data, some autocorrelation is likely. A more conservative analysis might cluster standard errors by industry or month. However, for this exploratory analysis, we proceed with IID errors and interpret results cautiously.

3. What has changed about who is working and earning income during the pandemic?

This analysis looks into how COVID-19 affected the weekly earning among employed individuals. Specifically looking at the retail sector and whether they had different earning trend compared to other industries. Retail workers tend to be lower paid and are more vulnerable to economic shocks. So looking at this sector would provide us with important insight on who is earning income in the post-COVID market.

For the first regression we used a Difference-in-Difference model to estimate how the earning in retail changed after COVID, relative to those that didn’t work in retail. With this method we where able to isolate the effect of the pandemic by comparing the changed two years prior and two years after the start of lock down.

THE DATA AND CLEANING PROCESS:

We use the current population survey micro data from IPUMS, cover the two years prior to COVID and two years post COVID. To clean the data we first converted the labelled variables to factor using haven: :as_factor, then filtered for observations with valid year, month, and work per hour data. Next, we defined employed individuals as those either “at work”, or “Has job, not at work last week.”. Afterwards, we had to be able to identify who the retail workers are. We did this by uploading the list of industry codes from canvas names “indnames.csv”. Afterwards, we had to define the post-COVID period as April 2020 to April 2022.

ANALYTICAL METHOD: DIFFERENCE-IN-DIFFERENCE

Retail is the binary variable equal to 1 for retail workers, PostCOVID is the variable indicator for the April 2020 onward. Retail x PostCOVID is the key interaction term, that tells us whether earning in retail changed differently than in other sectors after the start of the pandemic. Year and moth fixed effects control the broader time trends. The model is estimated using lm() in R to summarize the earnings data.

The coefficient on Retail x Post-COVID tells us the difference in earnings change for retail compared to other industries after April 2020. If the coefficient is -20, it suggests that retail workers earned $20 less per week than expected based on non-retail trends. Assuming the coefficient is negative and significant, the result would the indicate a disproportionate decline in the earning retail workers had after COVID. If positive or non-significant, retail earning may have remained steady or have a more rapid recovery.

We must be cautious when making this interpretation because earnings are imputed from hours worked and a fixed wage. Variation in actual hourly wages or benefits are not included!

ASSUMPTIONS AND LIMITATIONS

In this analysis we relied strongly on the following assumptions. Parallel trends with DiD we assumed that in the absence of COVID, earnings in retail and non-retail sectors would follow the same growth trajectory. No other concurrent shocks accrued, we assumed that no other events deferentially affected retail earnings after April 2020. We used a homogeneous wage rate, weekly earning were calculated assuming a $15 per hour pay for everyone, which then oversimplifies wage heterogeneity across the job and sectors. Lastly, we assumed aggregation bias, because we ran the model on averaged earnings by group-month. We lost individual-level variation and weighting, which could bias estimates.

Conclusion: The COVID-19 pandemic caused significant disruptions in the labor market, but its impact was far from uniform across industries. While retail employment showed some resilience in total job counts, retail workers still experienced notable declines in employment rates and earnings, reflecting the precarious nature of many retail jobs. The services sector, in contrast, endured the largest employment losses, illustrating the varied ways in which pandemic-related restrictions affected different parts of the economy.

These results highlight the complexity of economic recovery and the need for nuanced approaches when designing workforce support and economic policies. Simply looking at total job losses can mask deeper vulnerabilities faced by specific worker groups, particularly those in lower-wage and frontline positions. While the data and methods provide valuable insights, limitations such as wage imputation and potential unobserved heterogeneity suggest that continued monitoring and more granular research are essential.

Our study also points to the importance of ongoing data collection and analysis to better understand how employment and income patterns evolve as the economy adjusts post-pandemic.
